## Definition Of Genre

Policy memos are not like other academic papers. Their main purpose is to provide analysis and/or recommendations regarding a certain issue, and they are written for a specific, often limited, audience. Because of the need for quick, accurate information in the policy world, policy memos are written so that readers can efficiently access fact-based information in order to make an informed decision. Memos should therefore inform the audience in a concise, organized, and professional manner while still including the most relevant content.

## Writing Criteria For Policy Memos

An effective memo will do its job if the reader comprehends the main points after one quick read or even after reading just the first sentence of each section. To ensure that the memo gets the intended results, pay close attention to:
- content;
- structure;
- organization;
- word choice;
- clarity.

## Content

Content is the most important determinant of a good policy memo. Weak or illogical ideas, no matter how well presented, do not help the reader. A memo should provide both accurate and relevant information while also acknowledging the limitations of certain recommendations or analysis. Recommendations should include honest and realistic alternatives.

Key reminders:
- Present the most relevant information and state your main ideas and recommendations clearly.
- Present opinions as opinions, not as facts.
- Substantiate opinions.
- Use logic and facts to support each main point and to refute opposing points.
- Be accurate when citing facts in text.
- Avoid logical fallacies such as appeals to authority, slippery slope arguments, hasty generalizations, and faulty causation.

## Structure

### Header

Most memos take the general form of an email. The first page typically includes:
- `To:`
- `From:`
- `Date:`
- `RE:`

Example pattern:

```text
To: Timothy Geithner, Secretary of Treasury
From: Michelle (Min Eun) Jeon, Policy Advisor
Date: 2/20/2012
RE: Overcoming the Obstacle: House Speaker John Boehner
```

The header tells the reader:
- who the memo is for;
- what authority the writer has;
- the memo's most critical message.

### Executive Summary

Below the header, a memo generally includes an executive summary: a single paragraph that summarizes the entire memo. After reading that paragraph once, the reader should understand what the rest of the memo will explain.

Sample summary from the guide:

```text
Secretary Geithner's China currency bill depends on House Speaker John Boehner's support since the Speaker chooses which bill to debate in the House of Representatives. Mr. Boehner will only debate the bill after pressure from his financial supporters, security and investment corporations and their lobbyists; and his electoral constituents, Ohio State citizens and the Republican Party.
```

The guide emphasizes that a strong executive summary lets the audience quickly grasp:
- the immediate obstacle or decision point;
- the main actors who matter;
- what the rest of the memo will elaborate and support.

### Subheadings

The rest of the memo should have several sections elaborating on the points indicated in the executive summary. Those sections should begin with informative subheadings, usually in bold. A subheading should summarize the section so that the reader can skim it and still understand the essential message.

Examples:
- `Speaker Boehner: Greatest challenge for the currency bill`
- `Mr. Boehner's pro-business interests`

## Organization

Memo writers should employ the journalistic "inverted pyramid" style of writing. Place the most important information first, then follow with less significant detail in descending order of importance.

The guide contrasts this with an academic paper, where the most important point may come last. In a memo:
- the most important fact comes first;
- the same prioritization should occur in every section;
- large-scale structure and small subsections should both follow this logic.

## Word Choice

Word choice is critical for clarity and concision. Eliminate vague theoretical words and replace them with more concrete, specific terms.

Examples from the guide:

| Vague | More Concrete |
| --- | --- |
| facilitate | help / assist |
| indicate | say / show |
| concept | idea |

## Clarity

Policy memos must be clear and direct so readers can understand the main points quickly. If the content is strong, well organized, and phrased with well-chosen language, the result is a clear argument.

The guide contrasts memo writing with a typical history-paper introduction. Academic papers often:
- introduce the topic gradually;
- provide background detail first;
- end the introduction with the paper's purpose.

That pattern does not work well for a memo because it:
- lacks a central claim up front;
- provides too much detail in the beginning.

Instead, a memo should lead with central claims.

Example summary from the guide:

```text
Demands for a Solid Economic Policy Towards China
The demand for a policy to resolve the undervalued Chinese Yuan (renminbi) is increasing since the US public attributes the US' economic problems to the depreciation of the Chinese currency. The President and his new trade unit's immediate focus should be to get China to appreciate its artificially undervalued Yuan. Correcting the value of the Yuan will ultimately increase both on-shore jobs and US exports.
```

Why this works:
- context is limited to the first sentence;
- the recommendation appears immediately;
- the rest of the memo can provide supporting context and statistics later.

## Other Points To Clarify With The Instructor

The handout notes that instructors and teaching assistants may vary, so writers should clarify:
1. Should section titles be full sentences?
2. Should paragraphs and sections be divided into several parts, or should there only be a handful?
3. Is an executive summary required?
4. Are words such as `But`, `Because`, or `However` discouraged?
5. Should lengthy sentences be avoided?

The guide ends with one final reminder: read the prompt carefully, because the prompt often answers many of these format questions and reflects the grading criteria.
